Palmerins and Pigeonholes

Abstract

On an April afternoon, I went to Praça Francisco de Moraes with six microphones to record the sound of a pigeon. The square is named after the sixteenth-century author of the chivalric romance Palmerin of England. The publish date of that novel and the date of my recording are separated by almost five centuries, yet their contents share a structural logic: the pursuit of the encounter and the desire to capture.

This paper lets the grammar of field recording discourse and that of the heroic realm of the sixteenth-century chivalric romance leak into one another, allowing their logics to expose one another's failures to, and of, capture. It asks: What does the failure to capture reveal about the logic of capture embedded in the field recording practice, and what can speculative writing offer in its place?

My case study proposes the failure to capture as the fertiliser for new discourse/alternative imaginaries in field recording practices, the fiasco as the framework. The work consists of a binaural recording of a conventionally poorly executed field trip, accompanied by a booklet of diary entries interleaved with pages from the translated chivalric romance Palmerin of England.
